Research leadership

The founding physician serves as Principal Investigator on IM, geriatric, metabolic, and Alzheimer's-spectrum trials. The founding PMHNP serves as PI or sub-investigator on psychiatric, women's health, and behavioral trials (the PMHNP credential is critical and unlocks a wider trial portfolio than IM alone). A Clinical Research Coordinator (CRC) is the most important early hire — running day-to-day operations, regulatory submissions, and sponsor relationships.

Phoenix competitive read

Academic giants dominate the headline space. Commercial site landscape is fragmented, growing, and demonstrably fundable. Phoenix metro is a top-five US cluster for Alzheimer's, geriatric, and CNS trials. Full landscape in §03.

The Synergy
↓ Between Them
The wellness panel becomes the lowest-cost recruitment funnel a research site can operate. Industry sponsor recruitment costs run $1,500–$5,000 per enrolled patient; pulling from a pre-engaged panel cuts that to coordinator time alone — call it $200–$400 per enrolled patient. The research affiliation lifts wellness brand credibility in the longevity tier. Shared facility, EMR, and front-office overhead deliver $95K–$140K of annual cost avoidance vs. running two separate operations. This is the integration thesis in one paragraph.

§ 06 · Decision Guide

If the founder group can answer yes to these three questions in the same conversation, the choice becomes obvious.

Question 01
Is a Year-2 raise of $400–$700K realistic and committed?
If yes — Path B is on the table. If no or uncertain — Path A is the only honest version of the integrated thesis; the research side comes in Year 3 from cash flow. Don't pick B and hope for the raise.
Question 02
Do the founders want to run a research site themselves, or have research income without running a site?
If run it themselves — A or B. If income without operations — C. This is a real preference question, not a business question; the answer is about how founders want to spend their working hours over the next 5 years.
Question 03
Is the 5-year exit thesis required, or is steady cash-flowing practice ownership the goal?
If exit thesis required — B delivers the multiple-arbitrage exit; A delivers a smaller wellness-dominant exit; C delivers the smallest. If cash flow > exit — A or C both work and reduce risk meaningfully.
§ 07 · Year-2 Raise

If Path B is the choice, the raise is part of the Year-1 plan.

Path B only works if the founders can close $400K–$700K of follow-on capital by Month 12. That round will not be closeable without specific milestones, financial proof-points, and documentation prepared during Months 1–9. This is the readiness checklist.

The Year-2 raise is not a "we'll figure it out when we get there" line item. It is the single highest-leverage workstream in the Year-1 plan after the wellness launch itself. Friends-and-family rounds at this size close on three things: defensible numbers, a credible operator, and a use-of-funds the investor can model. The checklist below assumes the founders are raising from a combination of friends, family, individual angel healthcare operators, and possibly an SBA 7(a) loan for a portion. It does not assume institutional capital.

Months 1–3 · Foundation
The work that has to happen before the raise even starts
If this isn't done, nothing else in the checklist matters
  • Dual entity structure filed and operatingWellness PC + Research LLC both active, with separate bank accounts. Investor's first diligence question: "where would I actually invest?" The answer must be the Research LLC, not a vague "newco."
  • AKS / Stark compliance memo from healthcare regulatory counselWritten compliance program addressing inter-entity referrals, founder compensation flow, and patient consent. Investor diligence will ask for this; without it, the deal pauses.
  • Founders' personal financial disclosure preparedPersonal net worth statement, existing debt, expected founder capital contribution alongside the raise. Friends-and-family rounds require the founders to put in the first dollars.
  • Clean monthly P&L from Month 1 forwardQuickBooks Online + monthly close discipline. Investors will ask for trailing 9 months. If the books are messy, the round closes 60 days late.
Months 4–6 · Proof Points
The operational milestones that make the raise credible
Investors fund traction, not plans
  • ≥ 80 paying members across the wellness panelMonth 6 milestone. This is the inflection point investors look for — it proves the membership thesis is real, not theoretical. Tier 2 + Tier 3 mix above 25% is a bonus signal.
  • Monthly recurring revenue ≥ $35K by Month 6Implies the wellness side is on a $420K+ annualized trajectory. Below this number the raise becomes a "rescue round" psychologically; above it, it's a "growth round."
  • CRC candidate identified and pre-offeredNot hired yet (research isn't activated), but the candidate is known to the founders, the offer is drafted, and the start date is conditional on the raise closing. Demonstrates the use-of-funds is real.
  • Sponsor pipeline outreach initiated (3–5 active conversations)Outreach via warm intros — not signed CTAs, just confirmed sponsor or CRO conversations with documented next steps. Shows the research side has a real demand signal, not wishful thinking.
  • Patient retention ≥ 90% in Months 4–6Early-cohort retention is the canary in the coal mine. Concierge practices that lose 10%+ of charter members in the first 90 days have a brand or experience problem the raise won't fix.
Months 7–9 · Documentation
The data room and pitch materials
If a friends-and-family investor asks for it, it should already exist
  • 15-page investor memo / pitch deckDistilled version of this brief, tuned for the specific audience. Less narrative, more numbers. Include the wellness traction (Months 1–9 actuals), the research thesis with named therapeutic areas, the use-of-funds, and the 24-month milestone map.
  • Three-year financial model in ExcelLive model with assumption tabs, not a static PDF. Investors will want to flex member ramp, attrition, and study count themselves. Build it as if a CFO will pressure-test it — because one will.
  • Term sheet template draftedChoose a structure ahead of time: SAFE (common for early friends-and-family), convertible note (if you want a debt-like instrument), or priced preferred (more complex but cleaner). Have counsel review before the first investor sees it.
  • Personal references / advisory letters of intent (LOI)2–3 named clinical advisors, ideally with research site experience or healthcare PE familiarity. LOI doesn't need to be a contract — a one-page commitment to advise post-close is enough.
  • Use-of-funds memo with month-by-month deploymentWhere every dollar of the $400–$700K goes, by month, by category. Investors will not write a check against "research buildout — TBD." They will write a check against "$95K research equipment + CTMS in Months 13–14, CRC hire Month 13, regulatory & legal Months 13–15, working capital extension Months 13–18."
Months 10–12 · The Close
Closing the round and activating research
Six weeks of execution turns the readiness into capital
  • Soft-circle commitments ≥ 80% of target round sizeBefore formally opening the round, identify investors who have verbally committed dollar amounts. A $500K target round is closeable when $400K is soft-circled; below 60% soft-circled, delay the open.
  • SBA 7(a) loan application in progress (optional but accretive)If a portion of the $400–$700K can come as SBA debt rather than equity, founder dilution stays lower. Application takes 60–90 days; start by Month 9 to land funds by Month 12.
  • Legal documents and closing mechanics readySubscription agreements, investor questionnaires (accredited investor verification), wire instructions, cap table updated in Carta or similar. A round that drags through legal for 8 weeks loses momentum and investors.
  • Month 12 close · Month 13 deployment beginsCapital lands. CRC starts Month 13. Research equipment ordered Month 13. First sponsor CTA target moves from "aspirational" to "Month 14–18 commitment."
  • Investor reporting cadence establishedQuarterly updates to investors from Day 1 post-close. Not because it's required at this size, but because the next round (if needed) is easier with a 12-month track record of communication.
§ 08 · Convert A → B

Picking Path A doesn't close the door. It sequences the door.

If the wellness practice outperforms moderate-case projections through Months 12–18, Path A converts into Path B without a discrete capital event — the conversion is funded organically from accumulated cash flow. This appendix maps exactly what triggers the conversion, what it costs, and how to time it.

The conversion thesis is simple. Path A is Path B with the research activation deferred. If accumulated wellness cash flow at Month 18 exceeds $180K above plan, the founders can self-fund research activation without an external raise — or they can layer a smaller, less-dilutive raise on top of the cash flow. The five gates below define the conversion path. Each gate is a real go/no-go decision point with measurable criteria. If a gate's criteria aren't met, the founders pause, recalibrate, and re-evaluate at the next checkpoint — they don't force the conversion.

Gate 01 · Month 6 checkpoint
Wellness ramp is on or ahead of plan
The first signal that conversion is on the table. If member growth is on plan, retention is healthy, and Tier 2/3 mix is converting as modeled, the founders begin laying preparation work for Gate 02. If membership is behind plan, focus stays exclusively on wellness execution.
Pass criteria · all must be true
  • ≥ 80 paying members across all three tiers
  • ≥ 92% retention of charter cohort (first 30 members)
  • Tier 2 + Tier 3 mix ≥ 25% of total panel
  • Monthly recurring revenue ≥ $35K
Gate 02 · Months 7–12 · Pipeline pre-work
Sponsor outreach and CRC pre-identification
During this window the founders begin building the sponsor pipeline that activates research. This is unpaid work — no research revenue yet — but it positions the site so that Month 13 activation has a real pipeline to convert. The CRC candidate is identified but not yet hired.
Pass criteria · all must be true
  • 3–5 active sponsor conversations with documented next steps
  • At least 1 feasibility questionnaire returned
  • CRC candidate identified, informally pre-offered
  • Wellness panel ≥ 180 members; MRR ≥ $80K
  • Accumulated cash flow above plan ≥ $90K
Gate 03 · Month 12 · The conversion decision
Self-fund vs. raise vs. continue Path A
The fork. Three options on the table: (a) self-fund research activation from accumulated cash flow alone if the surplus is large enough; (b) self-fund partially and add a small $200–$300K friends-and-family round; (c) extend Path A another 6–12 months and re-evaluate at Month 18. This decision is binding for the next 6 months — once committed, the operational tempo changes.
Pass criteria · enables Gate 04
  • Accumulated cash flow above plan ≥ $180K (self-fund threshold)
  • OR accumulated surplus + soft-circled $200K+ friends-and-family
  • Wellness panel ≥ 250 members and trending up
  • ≥ 1 named sponsor with explicit interest in placing a study
  • CRC offer accepted, start date set for Month 13
Gate 04 · Months 13–18 · Activation
Research operations stand up · first CTA targeted
CRC starts. Research equipment ordered and installed. CTMS / eReg activated. Regulatory documentation prepared. Sponsor outreach intensifies. The wellness practice continues operating normally — founders do not reduce wellness time during this window; the CRC absorbs the research operational load. First site qualification visits expected Months 15–17.
Pass criteria · validates the conversion
  • CRC fully onboarded by Month 14
  • CTMS + eReg operational by Month 15
  • ≥ 2 site qualification visits completed by Month 17
  • First CTA signed or in active negotiation by Month 18
  • Wellness MRR continues to grow during activation period
Gate 05 · Months 19–24 · Stabilization
Second study activated · two-business operating cadence
The conversion is complete. The operating framework is now genuinely dual-business: wellness panel scaling toward 380+ members, first study enrolling, second CTA in negotiation. Founder time allocation rebalances: roughly 65% wellness clinical, 25% research PI activities, 10% business operations. The integration thesis is now operational rather than aspirational.
Pass criteria · conversion confirmed successful
  • First study actively enrolling patients
  • Second CTA signed or in late-stage negotiation
  • Wellness panel ≥ 320 members
  • Combined EBITDA positive on monthly basis
  • Investor reporting cadence established (if outside capital was raised)

Why this matters for the founder group decision

Picking Path A is not a downgrade of the integrated thesis — it's a conservative sequencing of the integrated thesis. The conversion path is real, the gates are measurable, and the capital required for conversion is meaningfully smaller than the upfront Path B raise. The founder group can pick A today, prove the wellness model through Months 1–12, and convert to B at Month 13–18 with materially better terms than they could raise today. The optionality is the point.

§ 09 · Risks

Five risks that apply to all three paths.

These are not path-specific. Whichever sequencing the founders choose, these are the items the group should agree on mitigations for before signing the lease LOI.

High
Membership ramp slower than modeled

All three paths assume ~180 members by end of Year 1. Concierge ramps in Phoenix metro have historically averaged 14–22 months to that milestone. Mitigation: Soft-launch with founder networks at 30 charter members; snowbird-season campaign (Oct–Apr); 12-month tier-conversion checkpoints with marketing pivot triggers.

High
Behavioral health integration gets diluted

The PMHNP credential is a primary differentiator across all three paths. If behavioral health becomes "an add-on for some members" instead of woven through every tier, the differentiation disappears. Mitigation: The founding PMHNP carries a defined psych panel from Month 1, with intake screening for every wellness member regardless of tier.

High
AKS / Stark compliance architecture under-built

Every path has compliance exposure — A defers it, B activates it Day 1, C makes it the core of the affiliation agreement. Mitigation: Two entities filed at Day 1 regardless of path; healthcare regulatory counsel engaged pre-launch; written referral protocol exists before the first patient is referred or the first CTA is signed.

Med
Founders try to wear every hat

At under-$500K Year-1 capital, hiring is thin. The temptation is for founders to absorb admin, marketing, billing, and operations as well as clinical. Mitigation: Defined founder roles in writing before launch. A part-time practice manager hire by Month 6, even if it means trimming founder draws.

Med
Lease terms lock the founders in

A 7-year fixed lease at the wrong rent is a $200K+ unforced error. Mitigation: Target 3+2+2 structure with renewal options; landlord TI allowance of $20–$30/sf for second-generation medical space; right of first refusal on adjacent suite for future expansion.

Low
Local economic softening

Phoenix metro household-income demographics in the target submarkets are strong but not immune to a broader downturn affecting self-pay willingness. Mitigation: Tier 1 pricing ($2,400/yr) sits below the regional concierge floor of $2,500; downside scenarios assume 35% slower ramp; lease has built-in flexibility above.
